ENBOX

In an increasingly digital world, pen and paper are still king to the educators, tutors, and students who teach and study English. Enbox is looking to transport the industry into the 21st century and beyond.

PROBLEM

ESL instructors continue to use mostly paper. This is ineffective, inefficient, and unsustainable. Most cloud-storage services also lack an effective filing system. Finally, most ESL products are aimed towards children. Nearly zero products target adult ESL learners and their teachers.

SOLUTION

A CLEAN, MINIMAL DASHBOARD

The main features are easily accessible through a clean and minimal dashboard, along with an organizational system based on tags and tasks.

DIGITIZED TESTS

Tests made on Enbox's platforms can be sent directly to students. Grading is automated and all the data is stored under each student's profile.

STUDENT LEARNING OBJECTVES

Enbox lists a set of skills for ESL learners from each level, from beginner to advanced, that serve as a guideline for teachers and objectives for students.

AUTOMATED ASSESSMENTS

Finally, Enbox's automated assessment tool gathers students' data and targets problem areas. It also graphs the students' assessments in comparison with the class average.

PROCESS

COMPETITIVE ANALYSIS

I conducted a SWOT analysis on Google Classroom, Dropbox, and Notion. Though these products offer quality services, an opportunity is open to catering to the world of adult ESL.

SWOT analysis

GOOGLE CLASSROOM

has a dizzying array of options for educators, but their services are geared towards younger generations. No content exists for ESL teachers and learners.

DROPBOX

has strong name recognition, competitive pricing, and ample storage space. It also targets small businesses, leaving space for a niche market to succeed.

NOTION

has trendy UI, clean interactivity, and templates such as lesson plans. They suffer from some complicated elements, such as uploading documents.

USER SURVEY

User research was focused on ESL teachers. I sent the surveys to current and former colleagues of mine. The following are some numbers that stood out.

93%

use Google Drive as their primary cloud service. Several reasons account for this, such as its ubiquity and relative ease of use

65%

cited “convenience” as the main factor in using their preferred cloud storage service. The ability to send and access documents through any device was key.

90%

wanted a product that allowed for the creation of original content. Teachers who offered more insight complained about a clear lack of organization in their cloud storage services.

I developed several variations of the dashboard, as you will see in "Prototype and testing". I designed a dashboard meant to display the various needs of each teacher from the get-go, including a schedule of upcoming assignments and a to-do list. This was the first iteration of what would become the final product.

PROTOTYPE + TESTING

The high-fidelity mockups went through several rounds of clickable prototypes and preference tests. After the first round, two main issues arose.

The first was that all the users were overwhelmed by the initial dashboard. It was too busy and users felt overwhelmed.

The second was that there was no clear hierarchy on the page. The most important aspect of the dashboard was the to-do list, but it wasn't obvious.

I went back to create a more prominent to-do list, one that teachers would find both convenient and well-organized.

VISUAL DESIGN

Enbox is essentially a filing system for instructors. This, in and of itself, isn’t the most exciting concept. Not only that, but most ESL products are often aimed towards children, replete with bright vibrant colors. I wanted to strike a balance between the professionalism of adult education with the youthfulness of a classroom.

We created a moodboard that would not only reflect a product that is neat and organized, but one that displays a brightness and levity that would light up even the dullest classroom.

COLOR

Enbox is not catered towards those who educate the young, but for those who work with adults. The brand aims to reflect the vivacity and energy that comes from being in a classroom. This dynamic dichotomy between young and sophisticated, playful and professional, is what Enbox represents.

LOGO

After some preliminary sketches, I was most drawn to the stack of three boxes. There were several components that aligned with the feel of Enbox.

First, was that the stacks emulated a stack of paper or folders. It also formed an "E", perfect for "Enbox". I took that idea and experimented with several iterations.

Unfortunately, there was something off about this design. It was just a little too....square. Then, with the help of a senior editor, a simple edit enhanced the logo. Instead of a stack of boxes atop one another, by flipping the logo by 90 degrees, it suddenly looked like a stack of binders.

This product promises to keep all of a teacher’s curriculum, lesson plans, quizzes, and grades in neat and pristine order in the cloud. No waste, less clutter, always accessible. It will be as if all their virtual papers are stashed away properly in its correct corresponding color-coded binder. The logo is representative of these virtual binders.

PREFERENCE TESTING

For the preference testing, I created several different versions of the dashboard to see what users responded to the most. While making the different iterations, I kept in mind the main issues that came to light during user testing: A busy dashboard with no clear hierarchy.

The first changes I made were of colors and different icons, thinking that these subtleties would help increase the visibility of the to-do list, which was the most important element on the page.

Users preferred the simpler icons, but still complained about the busyness of the page. My next step was to reduce the number of elements on the page.

I omitted the "Completed Assignments" section and extended "Student Snapshots" to include more useful stats that would help teachers track student's progress. Now, "Student Snapshots" was taking up the most real estate, taking away from the importance of the to-do list.

This time, I omitted the "Student Snapshot" and altered the to-do list to showcase different actions that were needed by the teacher. Though the teachers I tested preferred the new list by a large margin, they also wondered where the "Student Snapshot" went. More than half also mentioned the variety of colorful elements as being distracting.

After bumping my head on the table several times to clear my head, I implemented the changes and showcased the newest versions to the teachers. Luckily, they chose this version by a large margin (80%).

CONCLUSION

The most important lesson I learned from this project was one of flawed assumptions. By ignoring the data and emulating products already in the market, I was setting myself up for failure. As a beginner designer, I have read and been told that imitation is commonplace and an accepted part of the industry. It seems that I have to find the right balance between emulating the useful elements of others and also making my own.

This lesson also ties into the scope and purpose of the product. I’ve found that I lost sight of the original problem and the data that I gathered. I found myself adding and tweaking features of the visual design that weren’t of any importance or necessary because I was focused on the granular aspects rather than focusing on my . In the future, I will have to make sure I lay a solid foundation and stick to the fundamentals before I let my imagination steer me off-course.

For future projects, I will spend more time on the first steps of the design process (research, user stories, user flows, and wireframes) before even considering the overall visual design. The beginning of the journey will inform the later parts. I think what’s key here is being patient and focusing on solving the core problems first and foremost.
