Enbox

SUMMARY

Teaching English as a Second Language (ESL) is an industry stuck in amber. In an increasingly digital world, pen and paper are still king to the educators, tutors, and students who teach and study English. Enbox is looking to transport the industry into the 21st century and beyond.

PROBLEM

There is a three-fold problem.

First, enter any ESL institution and you will find bookshelves filled with binders filled with syllabi, lesson plans, and tests. This is ineffective, inefficient, and unsustainable.

Second, out of all the cloud-storage services available, neither have an effective organizational system. Files often get lost in the shuffle.

Third, though several products for ESL exist in the market, they are aimed chiefly towards children. Nearly zero products target adult ESL learners and their teachers.

SOLUTION

A 5-day scheduler so that the instructor is always aware of upcoming deadlines.

A To-do list, categorized by tasks such as corrections needed or assignments missing.

A student snapshot that displays each student’s progress.

PROCESS

SWOT ANALYSIS

I conducted a SWOT analysis on Google Classroom, Dropbox, and Notion. Two of these services are instantly recognizable, while the third is an up-and-comer trying to make its mark. Though these products offer quality services, an opportunity is open to catering to the world of adult ESL.

Google Classroom has a dizzying array of options for educators to enhance their classrooms. However, their scope is too broad, and they don't offer specific servies for ESL instructors.

Dropbox, like Google, has strong name recognition, competitive pricing, ample storage space, and convenient access to other products (like Google Drive). A specific weakness is Dropbox Paper, which is an application that allows users to create origianl content, but its UX isn't as smooth and intutive.

Notion is a relative newcomer to the market, with trendy UI, clean interactivity, and especially for educators, several templates such as lesson plans. They suffer from some needlessly complicated elements, such as its headache-inducing method of important documents from, say, Dropbox.

USER SURVEYS

User research was focused on ESL teachers. From the data, we were able to interpret their wants and needs. As a whole, their product choices and behavior were mostly uniform with little deviation. Here were some number that stood out:

93%

use Google Drive as their primary cloud service, especially because its ubiquity and compatibility with multiple platforms.

65%

cited “convenience” as the main factor in using their preferred cloud storage service, such as the ability to transfer documents between services.

90%

wanted a product that allowed for the creation of original content. This included uploading old content as well.

USER PERSONAS

From this data, I created two user personas. The first was Meg, a disciplined type-A instructor who values efficiency and organization. The second was Nicholas, a level-headed idealist who values simplicity and consistency. In my experience, from teaching English for nearly a decade, these personality-types were consistent (not to generalize, of course)

FAULTY ASSUMPTIONS

When I created my user stories, I made an erroneous assumption. I listed “As a new user, I want to create a new class” as a high-priority item. This was borne out of my competitive analysis, but not from the data I collected.

My user flows and wireframes reflected this as well. My user flows were centered around a product organized around classrooms.

Teachers were looking for convenience and quicker solutions, but the organization around the classroom was more hindering than it was useful.

Issues began to surface with the clickable prototype:

Would teachers scour each classroom for specific students or documents? Would it be easier to access their files straight from the dashboard? Of what value was organizing the product by classroom?

As the impracticalities of my original design surfaced, I knew where I had made my error: I had made an assumption of the needs of users based on my own personal thinking and from emulating the competitive landscape rather than using the data I collected.

USER STORIES + WIREFRAMES

I went back to the surveys and found that there were two common elements that nearly every user mentioned: convenience and a better system of organization. I went back to my user stories to reprioritize.

No longer was “creating a classroom” a high priority, or any priority.

For the wireframe, I designed a dashboard meant to display the various needs of each teacher from the get-go, including a schedule of upcoming assignments and a to-do list. This was the first iteration of what would become the final product.

VISUAL DESIGN

Enbox is essentially a filing system for instructors. This, in and of itself, isn’t the most exciting concept. Not only that, but most ESL products are often aimed towards children, replete with bright vibrant colors. I wanted to strike a balance between the professionalism of adult education with the youthfulness of a classroom.

We created a moodboard that would not only reflect a product that is neat and organized, but one that displays a brightness and levity that would light up even the dullest classroom.

COLOR

Enbox is not catered towards those who educate the young, but for those who work with adults. The brand aims to reflect the vivacity and energy that comes from being in a classroom. This dynamic dichotomy between young and sophisticated, playful and professional, is what Enbox represents.

This informed our choice of cool, pastel notes as the primary and secondary colors of the brand.

LOGO

After some preliminary sketches, I was most drawn to the a stack of three boxes. There were several components that aligned with the feel of Enbox.

First, was that the stacks emulated a stack of paper or folders. It also formed an "E", perfect for "Enbox". I took that idea and experimented with several versions.

Unfortunately, there was something off about this design. It was too....square. Then, with the help of a senior editor, a simple edit enhanced the logo.

Instead of a stack of boxes atop one another, by flipping the logo by 90 degrees, it suddenly looked like a stack of binders.

This product promises to keep all of a teacher’s curriculum, lesson plans, quizzes, and grades in neat and pristine order in the cloud. No waste, less clutter, always accessible. It will be as if all their virtual papers are stashed away properly in its correct corresponding color-coded binder. The logo is representative of these virtual binders.

TYPOGRAPHY

PROTOTYPE + TESTING

The high-fidelity mockups went through several rounds of clickable prototypes and preference tests. After the first round, two main issues arose.

The first was that all the users were overwhelmed by the initial dashboard.

The second was that there was no clear hierarchy on the page

This wouldn’t do. I went back to the drawing board to create a more prominent to-do list, one that teachers would find both convenient and well-organized.

CONCLUSION

The most important lesson I learned from this project was one of flawed assumptions. By ignoring the data and emulating products already in the market, I was setting myself up for failure. As a beginning designer, I have read and been told that imitation is commonplace and an accepted part of the industry. It seems that I have to find the right balance between emulating the useful elements of others and also making my own.

This lesson also ties into the scope and purpose of the product. I’ve found that I lost sight of the original problem and the data that I gathered (especially during the visual design). I found myself adding and tweaking features that weren’t of any importance or necessary in any way. In the future, I will have to make sure I lay a solid foundation and stick to the fundamentals before I let my imagination steer me off-course.

For future projects, I will spend more time on the first steps of the design process (research, user stories, user flows, and wireframes) before even considering the overall visual design. The beginning of the journey will inform the later parts. I think what’s key here is being patient and focusing on solving the core problems first and foremost.
