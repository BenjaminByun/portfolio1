ENBOX

In an increasingly digital world, pen and paper are still king to the educators, tutors, and students who teach and study English. Enbox is looking to transport the industry into the 21st century and beyond.

Problem

The problem is three-fold

First, ESL instructors continue to use mostly paper. This is unsustainable.

Second, most cloud-storage services lack an effective organizational system for teachers.

Finally, most ESL products are aimed towards children, ignoring the world of adult ESL.

Solution

Beautifully minimal UI

Clean and crisp, Enbox is smooth, intuitive, and materially professional.

Digitized testing

All tests, including essays, can be made in Enbox and sent directly to students.

Student learning objectives

A list of level-specific skills guides students to improve and informs teachers for their lesson plans.

Automated assessments

Grades are automated, analyzed, and recorded for both teachers and students. No red pen necessary.

Research

Competitive analysis

I conducted a SWOT analysis on Google Classroom, Dropbox, and Notion. Though these products offer quality services, an opportunity is open to catering to the world of adult ESL.

Click here for full SWOT analysis
GOOGLE CLASSROOM

STRENGTHS: Free (mostly), myriad tools for educators (including VR), ease of assigning and assessing student work

WEAKNESSES: Mainly targets K-12 school districts, no ESL-specific resources

OPPORTUNITIES: ESL-friendly features would expand its appeal to the worldwide ESL market

THREATS: Smaller competitors (such as Ellevation) offer better services for ESL teachers

DROPBOX

STRENGTHS: Name recognition, competitive pricing, easy to move/share/categorize files and folders

WEAKNESSES: Dropbox Paper UI isn't as smooth as Google Docs

OPPORTUNITIES: Targeting and catering to a broader audience will pit it more directly against Google Drive

THREATS: Niche offerings from smaller competitors with unique features can steal base customers away.

NOTION

STRENGTHS: Aesthetically pleasing, plenty of interactivity, includes templates specifically for teachers

WEAKNESSES: Importing documents isn't a simple process, interface takes getting used to

OPPORTUNITIES: Offering more teacher-targeted content would appeal to school districts

THREATS: Appealing to broader audiences may result in losing smaller markets

User personas

I interviewed colleagues of mine and created two user personas.

MEGAN

English Teacher

34

GOALS

Be more digitally organized with her documents

FRUSTRATIONS

Losing track of files and recreating the same documents again

MOTIVATIONS

Megan is the most organized teacher in her school. She has binders of lesson plans and activites that she's collected over the years. The main reason she hasn't switched to digital is that she can't stand the disorganization of her current digital system, so she sticks to paper for now.

I would switch to 100% digital tomorrow, but paper is so much easier for prep work, organization, and grading.

NICHOLAS

English Teacher

27

GOALS

Improve his teaching, have more time to focus on students

FRUSTRATIONS

Rushing through corrections, difficulty finding specific files

MOTIVATIONS

Nicholas is an excellent teacher. He cares deeply about his job and his students, even the slack-jawed yokels. He wants to have more time and focus for individual students, but with the surfeit of lesson plans, activities, corrections, and assessments on his plate, he has little time much else.

If I had a better system, I would have more time to teach my students gerunds verus infinitives.

Information architecture

User stories

As I prioritized the navigational and actionable elements of my product, the classroom was always front and center.

During initial wireframe testing, more than half the teachers mentioned wanting quicker access to their files. I realized that I didn't make "I want to create a classroom" based on any data, but rather an assumption I had made about the needs of teachers.

It made sense to me, being a teacher for over 6 years, but it wasn't optimal for this product. I revisited my userstories and made the simple yet necessary adjustments.

HIGH PRIORITY

I want to create classrooms

I want to create new assignments/ documents/ spreadsheets

I want to upload files and folders from my personal devices

I want quick and easy access to my files

MEDIUM PRIORITY

I want to import documents from my computer

I want to organize files efficiently

I want to organize files conveniently

I want to tag files

LOW PRIORITY

I want a reminder for upcoming tasks

I want to share files with others

I want others to share files with me

USER FLOWS

My initial user flows also reflected a classroom-centric setup.

All roads went through the classroom. This also proved to be a simple fix... I eliminated classrooms altogether.

Wireframing

After getting rid of my classroom-first theory, I developed a dashboard with a task-based focus. It allowed for quick access to high-priority files which would be both time-efficient and convenient.

Click here for Wireframes

From sketch to wireframe, the to-do list was always just a small part of the dashboard. In testing, this would prove to be problematic. 75% of teachers would express confusion at the lack of information hierarchy. As you will see in the mockups, the to-do list was later transformed to have a more prominent presence.

MOCKUPS

The to-do list got a huge upgrade from the original wireframe on the left, to the newer version on the right. Now there was no question about what was most important.

Testing

The high-fidelity mockups went through several rounds of clickable prototypes and preference tests. The most prominent issue emerged when users were tasked with making a test.

Click here for prototype

The task was to create a quiz with two questions. Though users understood how to make questions, over 50% expressed confusion over the navigation of the test. They didn't understand that the left column was the navbar, so to speak.

By omitting the "add question" button and instead creating the question outline, more users then understood that they could switch between questions from the left-hand navigation.

Visual design

STYLE GUIDE

Enbox is essentially a filing system for instructors. I wanted to strike a balance between the professionalism of adult education with the youthfulness of a classroom.

We created a moodboard that would not only reflect a product that is neat and organized, but one that displays a brightness and levity that would light up even the dullest classroom.

Click here for style guide
COLOR

Enbox is not catered towards those who educate the young, but for those who work with adults. The brand aims to reflect the vivacity and energy that comes from being in a classroom.

This informed our choice of cool, pastel notes as the primary and secondary colors of the brand.

LOGO

After some preliminary sketches, I was most drawn to the stack of three boxes. There were several components that aligned with the feel of Enbox.

This product promises to keep all of a teacher’s curriculum, lesson plans, quizzes, and grades in neat and pristine order, as if the virtual papers are filed away in its correct corresponding color-coded binder. The logo is representative of these binders.

TYPOGRAPHY

PREFERENCE TESTING

Test #1

The first element I tested was the to-do list. I had initially envisioned that icons would represent the task that needed doing, e.g. exclamation point = high-priority item.

B was the unanimous winner. The icons were more straight-forward, and organizing by tasks was much more practical than having disparate tasks listed together.

Test #2

The next test was about differentiating between levels.

In this case, it was a 50/50 split between A and B. I ended up choosing B because of its minimal design, though A would certainly have evinced more levity.

Conclusion

The most important lesson I learned from this project was one of flawed assumptions. By ignoring the data and emulating products already in the market, I wasn't making decisions grounded in data.

This lesson also ties into the scope and purpose of the product. I’ve found that I lost sight of the original problem and the data that I gathered. In the future, I will lay a solid foundation and stick to the fundamentals before I let my imagination steer me off-course.

For future projects, I will spend more time on the first steps of the design process (research, user stories, user flows, and wireframes) before even considering the overall visual design. The beginning of the journey will inform the later parts. I think what’s key here is being patient and focusing on solving the core problems first and foremost.
