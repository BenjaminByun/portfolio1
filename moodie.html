PROBLEM

What you cook is limited by two major factors: what you want and what you have.

What do you want?

Moods are fickle. They can be as specific as an ingredient, or as vague as a texture. Googling these cravings yields thousands of results. The paralysis of choice

What do you have?

Not only that, but the results you receive aren’t always practical, with recipes calling for ingredients you never even heard of. FenuGreek leaves? Aceto balsamico tradizionale? Quinoa? Finding the perfect recipe is as random as betting on double 0 in roulette.

SOLUTION

MOBILE KITCHEN

Input ingredients and find recipes that don't require extra shopping.

ADVANCED FILTERS

Narrow your options based on your mood.

MISE EN PLACE

Less clutter and limited choice means easier choosing.

ALL THE FAT TRIMMED

Find ingredients and instructions without sifting through the backstory or history.

Research

Competitive analysis

Yummly, RecipeKeeper, and Epicurious were all popular apps. Yummly and Epicurious are keepers of thousands of recipes, with users having access to them all through their apps. RecipeKeeper is a way of storing your own recipes, a personalized cookbook at it were.

Click here to see the full SWOT analysis
YUMMLY

STRENGTHS: Thousands of recipes, beautiful pictures, smooth onboarding, customizable

WEAKNESSES: Overwhelming, bloated, claustrophobic, paralysis of choice

OPPORTUNITIES: Incorporating an ingredient input would enhance user's selection

THREATS: An app with less complexity and higher functionality would steal market share

RECIPE KEEPER

STRENGTHS: Ease of inputting recipes, clean UI, intuitive, useful meal-planning function

WEAKNESSES: Basic UI, limited functionality

OPPORTUNITIES: Automatic scheduling of meals might be a useful feature

THREATS: The success of the app depends on the motivation of users to always be inputting ingredients

EPICURIOUS

STRENGTHS: Thousands of recipes, easy to read, known brand

WEAKNESSES: Fewer instances of personal customization, too many ads, recipes with zero ratings

OPPORTUNITIES: A quality control of recipes would elevate user confidence

THREATS: Too much bloat makes the app feel impersonal, as if it's just an app-version of the website (which it is)

USER FLOWS

The most challenging aspect of the flow was figuring out how to get users to input their ingredients.

This process is burdensome and time-consuming, two killers of user interest. Originally, I had incorporated this step as part of the onboarding process.

In this flow, the red path represents inputting ingredients. Users had the option of skipping it altogether.

If the user chooses to go through the onboarding process, it's evident that there's no quick and easy way to get through this. As I would learn, this task was much too daunting, even if optional, as part of an onboarding process.

The new flow would eventually get rid of this linear progression of adding ingredients, and put it all into one screen.

WIREFRAMING

I fleshed out the process of inputting ingredients into wireframes. As you can see, it was a bit much.

Click here to see the wireframes

Another challenge was how to incorporate two high-level but distinct actions. Adding ingredients and filtering the recipes were equally important. But how to incorporate it without clutter? All the recipe apps I’ve browsed felt too busy and claustrophobic. This may have been okay for their purposes, but I wanted my app to be as simple and clean as possible.

My original solution was to incorporate a floating action button, or FAB for short. In my original wireframes, I experimented with the design of using a FAB. As you will see in my mockups, I took this idea a step further.

MOCKUPS

Though there were a few apps that factored in ingredients into finding recipes, they were not well known. I wanted to set Moodie ahead of the pack.

Click here to see the mockups

I was still determined to make the onboarding process work. I hypothesized that by making the onboarding process into a type of whack-a-mole game in which users just touch the ingredients they have, the process would be more enjoyable.

The idea of a floating action "MOOD" button was not only useful, but unique. I was feeling bold and decided to incorporate two FABs: the aforementinoed MOOD button and a simpler ADD button to input ingredients. Both were of equal importance but completely separate.

TESTING

I tested the prototype with several participants. The two solutions I came up with in my high-fidelity mockups, the onboarding process and the FAB buttons, both ran into problems.

Click here to view prototype

ONBOARDING TROUBLES

Upon testing, the onboarding process had a success rate of 0%. Yep. There were several issues.

First was the suddenness of this daunting task. As the first step after signing in, there was a bit too much to do without a proper introduction to the app.

Next was the lack of progression. Because inputting ingredients wasn’t meant to be a clearly linear process. As a result, users were confused with what to do after inputting ingredients. Though there were breadcrumbs in the original wireframes, those have since been omitted because of their perceived lack of importance.

I had to omit this process from the onboarding. But then when would users input their ingredients? Most of the app’s value comes from its ability to give results based on what’s already in the user's kitchens.

The solution was to offer only an informational onboarding that lets users know the importance of inputting ingredients. Then on the dashboard, have a salient call-to-action so users can easily input ingredients at any point.

The input wizard was also omitted to create a list of broad categories that most ingredients fall under. Users can access them by simply clicking on the category, and then they’d be able to touch each ingredient to add it, and touch it again to delete it. Success rate went from 0 to 100.

MOOD SWINGS

The mood button was confusing and unintuitive to users.

It wasn't exactly clear what the mood button did and how it worked. And though 40% of participants enjoyed the look and feel of it, most did not care much for it.

The solution? A filter button. It is an intuitive icon, one that is common and is usually located in the top-right corner. Though it no longer had the flash of a mood button, it was simple, practical, and easily understood by 100% of participants.

VISUAL DESIGN

I wanted Moodie to appeal to more seasoned home cooks. I opted for darker tones, creating a more sophisticated vibe. All other visual choices followed this theme.

PREFERENCE TESTING

The first preference test I performed had to do with the images of food. The images of the food is just as important as the food itself, according to several of survey participants. I wanted to test how big users liked their images.

Within these choices, B was the clear winner. It provided bigger pictures on cards that showed more relevant information. Details were also much easier to read.

CONCLUSION

I learned two important lessons from this product.

The first was this: Don't expect the user to do any heavy lifting right away. Even the most enthuastic of users will lose interest real quick. To put it another way...it's a total mood-killer.

The second lesson is one that's easily forgotten in the midst of ideating: Be practical. I wanted Moodie to stand out from the competition. A floating action MOOD button seemed like a perfect idea. I cherised the idea like Gollum and his ring, so it was a rude awakening when most users couldn't make sense of it. I had a choice: I could hold onto the idea as it fell into the depths of Mordor...or I could come up with a sensible solution.

A filter button was the sensible solution. No pizzazz, maybe even pedestrian, but it worked.

Don’t try to reinvent the wheel. Yet.
