MOODIE

Every night, across millions of households, the same perennial conundrum comes up before every meal: What are you in the mood for? It could be as vague as a cuisine (“I’m feeling Chinese!”) or as specific as one ingredient (“We should probably use the 10 pounds of potatoes we got last month,”). Moodie manages your moods and gives you recipes of exactly what you didn’t know you wanted, all according to the ingredients that you already have!

PROBLEM

What you cook is limited by two major factors: what you want and what you have.

What do you want?

Moods are fickle. They can be as specific as an ingredient, or as vague as a texture. Googling these cravings yield thousands of results. The paralysis of choice

What do you have?

Not only do you have the paralysis of choice, but the results you receive aren’t always practical, with recipes calling for ingredients you never even heard of. FenuGreek leaves? Aceto balsamico tradizionale? Quinoa? Finding the perfect recipe is as random as betting on double 0 in roulette.

SOLUTION

MOBILE KITCHEN

By inputting the ingredients in their kitchens, users will be able to sort through their options based on what they already have. No more shopping for one extra ingredient!

ADVANCED FILTERS

Whatever mood one might be in, be it a strong craving for a chocolate or something crunchy, Moodie provides filters to narrow down your options.

MISE EN PLACE

By limiting the number of ingredients per screen, users will be prevented from browsing thousands of recipes aimlessly.

ALL THE FAT TRIMMED

Each recipe page will indicate tags to match whatever filter you put on, instructions that are stripped to the bone, and possible substitutions for any ingredients you might be missing.

INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE

USER FLOWS

The most challenging aspect of the flow was figuring out how to get users to input their ingredients.

This process is burdensome and time-consuming, two killers of user interest. Originally, I had incorporated this step as part of the onboarding process.

In this flow, you can see that the user has the chance to skip the onboarding wizard altogether to go straight to the dashboard.

If the user chooses to go through the onboarding process, it's evident that there's no quick and easy way to get through this. As I would learn, this task was much too daunting, even if optional, as part of an onboarding process.

The new flow would eventually get rid of this linear progression of adding ingredients, and put it all into one screen.

WIREFRAMES

In my previous project, the lesson I learned was to set a strong foundation before considering the visual design. I did this beginning with the wireframes, where I carefully followed the tenets of material design before any visual input. This has helped the progression of my project by miles.

Another challenge was how to incorporate two high-level but distinct actions

Adding ingredients and filtering the recipes were equally important. But how to incorporate it without clutter? All the recipe apps I’ve browsed felt too busy and claustrophobic. This may have been okay for their purposes, but I wanted my app to be as simple and clean as possible.

My original solution was to incorporate a floating action button, or FAB for short. In my original wireframes, I experimented with the design of using a FAB. As you will see in my mockups, I took this idea a step further.

MOCKUPS

I wanted Moodie to stand out from the competition.

Though there were a few apps that factored in ingredients into finding recipes, they were not well known. I wanted to find a way to set Moodie ahead of the pack.

I was still determined to make the onboarding process work. I hypothesized that by making the onboarding process into a type of whack-a-mole game in which users just touch the ingredients they have, the process would be more enjoyable.

I decided to take this idea of an FAB and incorporate two onto the dashboard. The idea of a "MOOD" button was not only useful, but unique. Moodie would stand out from its rivals with its signature "MOOD" button! I was feeling bold and decided to incorporate two FABs: the aforementinoed MOOD button and a simpler ADD button to input ingredients. Both were of equal importance but completely separate.

PROTOTYPE + TESTING

I tested the prototype with several participants. The two solutions I came up with in my high-fidelity mockups both ran into problems.

View prototype
ONBOARDING TROUBLES

Upon testing, the onboarding process had a success rate of 0%. Yep. There were several issues.

First was the suddenness of this daunting task.

As the first step after signing in, there was a bit too much to do without a proper introduction to the app.

Next was the lack of progression.

Because inputting ingredients wasn’t meant to be a clearly linear process. As a result, users were confused with what to do after inputting ingredients. Though there were breadcrumbs in the original wireframes, those have since been omitted because of their perceived lack of importance.

I had to omit this process from the onboarding. But then when would users input their ingredients? Most of the app’s value comes from its ability to give results based on what’s already in the user's kitchens.

The solution was to offer only an informational onboarding that lets users know the importance of inputting ingredients. Then on the dashboard, have a salient call-to-action so users can easily input ingredients at any point.

The input wizard was also omitted to create a list of broad categories that most ingredients fall under. Users can access them by simply clicking on the category, and then they’d be able to touch each ingredient to add it, and touch it again to delete it. Success rate went from 0 to 100.

MOOD SWINGS

The mood button was confusing and unintuitive to users.

It wasn't exactly clear what the mood button did and how it worked. And though 40% of participants enjoyed the look and feel of it, most did not care much for it.

The solution? A filter button. It is an intuitive icon, one that is common and is usually located in the top-right corner. Though it no longer had the flash of a mood button, it was simple, practical, and easily understood by 100% of participants.

VISUAL DESIGN

I wanted Moodie to appeal to more seasoned home cooks. I opted for darker tones, creating a more sophisticated vibe. All other visual choices followed this theme.

PREFERENCE TESTING

The first preference test I performed had to do with the images of food. The images of the food is just as important as the food itself, according to several of survey participants. I wanted to test how big users liked their images.

Within these choices, B was the clear winner. It provided bigger pictures on cards that showed more relevant information. Details were also much easier to read.

CONCLUSION

When I first designed the app, I was excited about a design feature that would stand out amongst other apps: the MOOD button. I had seen the floating action button be put to good use in complicated apps such as My Fitness Pal, to much success. FABs are clear, salient, and offer a high-end function without cramping up the UX of the rest of the app.

The problem was that it wasn’t practical. When users clicked on the mood button, a list of options would show up such as “cuisine” or “cooking time”. Once they chose one, the user would be taken to a screen where they could choose one or more from a list of options in order to narrow down their results.

But surely, users would want to input more than one filter at any given moment. What if they wanted a meal within 15 minutes that also happened to be Korean? From how the initial user flow developed, this would create an extra step that made the Mood button obsolete. Realizing that it was just an advanced filter option, I opted to make it a simple filter function. Though it didn’t have the flash or appeal of a FAB, it was simple and intuitive that all the users understood. right away.

My biggest takeaway from this was this: Don’t try to reinvent the wheel. Yet.
